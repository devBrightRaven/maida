Two Faces

The names are not decoration

Maida has two modes, drawn from the vocabulary of Japanese martial arts. Their sequence is intentional: you must prepare before you can face what appears.

臨

Rin

to face what appears

One game. TRY or NOT NOW. No comparison, no browsing, no "what if the next one is better." You stand before a single world and decide whether to enter.

In kendo, nozomu is the moment you face your opponent. In the nine-syllable cut (kuji-in), Rin is the first syllable -- representing the strength to confront what is before you.
構

Kamae

quiet preparation

Curate your shelf. Organize games into named groups called Kata. Decide which worlds are worth facing today. This is not browsing -- it is preparing your stance.

Kamae is the ready stance before drawing the sword. In tea ceremony, it is the quiet arrangement of space before the guest arrives. No kamae, no rin. You must prepare before you can face.
臨兵闘者皆陣列在前

The nine-syllable cut. Rin is the first -- the strength to begin. Maida exists in that first syllable: the space between hesitation and entry.

How it works

Simpler than you expect

1

Open Maida

It scans your locally installed Steam games. Nothing leaves your device.

2

One game appears

Selected by weighted probability based on your past choices. Not a recommendation -- a reflection.

3

TRY or NOT NOW

Willing to enter this world? TRY launches it. Not feeling it? NOT NOW shows you another. That's it.

4

Maida disappears

After you choose, the screen freezes. No "play next." No notifications. The tool dies the moment it fulfills its purpose.

Most game launchers help you browse.
Browsing creates decision fatigue.
Maida removes browsing entirely.

One game. One choice. Move on.
-- Design Constitution, Section 1.13

What Maida refuses to do

The moat is restraint

No recommendations. No tracking playtime. No streaks, achievements, or "you did great today." No notifications to bring you back. No social features. No data uploaded anywhere. All behavioral data stays on your machine.

The ideal user is someone who, one day, no longer needs Maida. If the tool becomes "very smart" or "very considerate," it has failed. Maida exists not to improve you, but to not stand between you and the world you want to enter.
